{{Politics of Bolivia}}

'''Municipalities in [[Bolivia]]''' ({{langx|es|municipios}}) are [[Administrative divisions of Bolivia|administrative divisions]] of the entire national territory governed by [[local elections]]. Municipalities are the third level of administrative divisions, below [[Departments of Bolivia|departments]] and [[Provinces of Bolivia|provinces]]. Some of the provinces consist of only one municipality. In these cases the municipalities are identical to the provinces they belong to. There are 340 municipalities.

==History of governance==
Municipalities in Bolivia are each led by a mayor, an executive office. Mayors were appointed by the national government from 1878 to 1942 and from 1949 to 1987.<ref name=WB-BC13>{{Citation | publisher = World Bank | last = Burki | first = Shahid Javed |author2=Guillermo E. Perry |author3=William R. Dillinger | title = Beyond the center: Decentralizing the State | date = 1999-07-31 | page=13 }}</ref> Local elections were held under the 1942 municipal code, which was in force until 1991.<ref name=WB-BC13 /> The 1985 Organic Law of Municipalities restored local elections for mayor and created a legislative body, the municipal council.<ref>{{Cite journal | volume = XI | issue = 20 | pages = 61–95 [68] | last = Córdova | first = Eduardo | title = Cochabamba es el centro es la ausencia: Impulsos estatales y sociales de la descentralización en Cochabamba (1994–2008) | journal = Decursos: Revista de Ciencias Sociales | year = 2009 }}</ref>

In 1994, the entire territory of Bolivia was merged into municipalities, where previously only urban areas were organized as municipalities. As an effect of [[decentralization]] through the 1994 [[Law of Popular Participation]] the number of municipalities in Bolivia has risen from an initial twenty-four (in 1994) to 327 (in 2005), to 337 (at the time of the [[Bolivian regional election, 2010|2010 elections]]),<ref name=Castel2010>Jorge Castel, "[http://www.la-razon.com/versiones/20100328_007045/nota_257_979891.htm 10 nuevos municipios elegirán a sus autoridades en los comicios] {{webarchive|url=https://web.archive.org/web/20100331134913/http://www.la-razon.com/versiones/20100328_007045/nota_257_979891.htm |date=2010-03-31 }}," ''La Razón'', 29 Marzo 2010.</ref><ref>[http://www.bolivia.com/noticIAS/AutoNoticias/DetalleNoticia42293.asp www.bolivia.com]</ref> to 339 ({{as of|lc=y|August 2010}}).<ref name=Aug2010>{{Cite news | title = Se crean dos nuevos municipios en La Paz | work = Los Tiempos | access-date = 2012-01-02 | date = 2010-08-09 | url = http://www.lostiempos.com/diario/actualidad/politica/20100809/se-crean-dos-nuevos-municipios-en-la-paz_84269_160598.html | url-status = dead | archive-url = https://web.archive.org/web/20150518065547/http://www.lostiempos.com/diario/actualidad/politica/20100809/se-crean-dos-nuevos-municipios-en-la-paz_84269_160598.html | archive-date = 2015-05-18 }}</ref> Of the 327 municipalities existing after 2005, 187 are inhabited by a mainly [[Indigenous peoples of the Americas|indigenous]] population; 184 of these are located in the five Andean departments, with the remaining three in Santa Cruz department.<ref>According to a definition where a majority identify with an indigenous people and speak an indigenous language, with a two-thirds majority meeting looser criteria. {{Cite book | publisher = Vicepresidencia del Estado Plurinacional | last = Albó | first = Xavier |author2=Carlos Romero | title = Autonomías Indígenas en la realidad boliviana y su nueva Constitución | location = La Paz | date = April 2009 | page = 22}}</ref> New municipalities must have at least 10,000 residents, or 5,000 in the case of border areas.<ref name=Castel2010 />
